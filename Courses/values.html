Values

There is no yearning more important to human beings than to freely pick and pursue our life direction. Clear sense of self directed meaning provides us with an essentially inexhaustible supply of motivation. But we can easily lose sight of what is actually meaningful to us, pursuing socially compliant goals and superficial gratifications instead.

Why do we lose sight of what is meaningful?

We misdirect our yearning for meaning for a number of reasons. For example:

  • We don't trust ourselves to make good choices, and we escape from the freedom life gives us.
  • We fear we might pick a life course we don't have the necessary qualities to pursue.
  • We worry that our values may be out of step with cultural norms, leading us to be looked down on, left out, or even ridiculed.
  • Most commonly of all, we turn away from our true values because of past pain we want to avoid.

In the main, our culture doesn't help people choose their own sense of meaning. instead, we are encouraged to define what gives us a sense of worth by chasing superficial ones. We mistake a quick hit of gratification for a sense of meaning, and we accumulate things and achievements, pursuing a long list of socially mandated "shoulds". dominant social messages that are worth its evaluated by our possessions and by culturally approved forms of achievement or compliance with social expectations, whether that's work success, getting married and having children, or even being happy.

Consider the effects of materialism, the belief that possessions and their acquisition will lead to life satisfaction. Studies have been done in which people fill out surveys asking if they agree with statements such as "some of the most important achievements in life including acquiring material possessions", "I'd be happier if I could afford to buy more things"; and "I like to own things that impress people." Agreement correlates significantly with anxiety, depression, negative self-assessment, and low life satisfaction.

The values skill allows us to redirect our yearning for meaning toward the pursuit of the activities that align with what we truly find meaningful.

Using Our Acceptance, Defusion, Self, and Presence Skills to Assist Pivoting Towards Our Values

Acceptance Allows Us to Listen

If we're blocked from connecting to our values because we're trying to avoid pain, we ironically only contribute to our pain. By listening to our pain instead, we can identify the discrepancies between the way in which we're living and the way we want to live. When we open up to pain, we should flip it over and ask "what would I have to not care about for this not to hurt?". In our pain we find our values, and in our avoidance we find our values disconnection. Without emotional flexibility and openness, it's impossible to live according to chosen values.

Presence Helps Stay Focused on the Ongoing Journey

It is important to distinguish between goals and values. Values are qualities of living - such as living lovingly, playfully, kindly, compassionately, protectively, persistently, and faithfully. Values-based living makes goals meaningful, rather than goals being valuable in themselves. Goals are in the future until they are achieved, and then they're quickly in the past. Values are always in the now. Living day to day according to our values is enormously rewarding. As we work toward living in accordance with our values, attention to the present helps focus on our current behavior - on the journey rather than reaching the destination.

The Valued Living Questionnaire

The VLQ asks a series of questions about what your values are and how much you have been living in accordance with them, evaluating that measure in a set of life domains, on a scale of 1 to 10. Answer as honestly as possible, setting aside as best you can social pressures and the wagging mental fingers of should and have to.

There are a number of ways to use the results. The first is to look at all domains that have relatively high important scores (a score of 9 or 10 ) and also have relatively low consistency scores (6 or less). These are clear problem areas, and I suggest doing your initial values work with any of them. Then you can move on to other areas.

The overall score can also be useful. To get a rough sense of how your score compares to those of the broad public, the average composite result is 61.

Now, with a good idea of the values domain that you'd like to start working on, you're ready to get going.

Exercise 1: Values Writing

Write for 10 minutes about a value you care about deeply in any domain from the list above. As you do so, address the following questions: What do I care about in this area? What do I want to do in this area that reflects caring? When in my life has this value been important? What have I seen in my life when other pursue thsi value, or not? What might I do to manifest this value more in my life? When have I violated this value and has that been costly?

Try to focus your writing on the qualities of your life as you want to live it - qualities of your own that you hold as being of intrinsic importance. This is between you and you; it's not about seeking approval or following a bunch of rules.

Now read what you wrote and see if you can distill out of it a few examples of things you want to do in this area. Actual behavior. Next look for mentions of the qualities you want to manifest in your actions. You might want to do things genuinely, lovingly, carefully, creatively, curiously, compassionately, respectfully, openly, joyously, industriously, healthfully, adventurously and so on and so on.

With this first set of actions you'd like to take, you might want to move now to the next course, to gain guidance about how to commit to them. If you move on to the next course be sure to come back to do at least these next two exercises. They have proven very powerful for people in developing a deeper awareness of their values and continuing to chart a course of more meaningful living.
